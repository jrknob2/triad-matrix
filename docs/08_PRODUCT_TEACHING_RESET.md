# Product / Teaching Reset

> Historical note:
> This document captures the earlier teaching reset that led into the current product spec work.
> It is not an active screen or flow contract, and references to onboarding reflect an abandoned path.

## Purpose

This document resets the product around a clearer teaching philosophy.

It does three things:

1. summarizes relevant drumming knowledge from reputable education sources
2. turns that knowledge into a teaching model for this app
3. critiques the current product and proposes a plan to bring it back into alignment

The intent is to stop designing features in isolation and instead build a coachable learning system.

## Source Facts

This section is factual source-backed material. It is not yet product recommendation.

### 1. Rudiments and stroke types are foundational, not optional

Percussive Arts Society treats the 40 International Drum Rudiments as a standard reference and explicitly frames them as core technical material. PAS resources also emphasize the four basic stick motions and the importance of mastering stickings and rudiments as transferable fundamentals rather than isolated exercises.

Vic Firth's rudiment curriculum reinforces this same idea in a practical way:

- progress is tracked by level
- rudiments are practiced `open-close-open`
- application exercises are part of the learning loop
- learners are advised not to move through the list in a flat sequence, but through a guided tiered progression

Implication:

- a good app in this space should not just catalog patterns
- it should teach progression, stroke control, and transfer into application

### 2. Rudiments matter because they improve transferable drum-set skills

Drumeo's rudiment guidance explicitly connects rudimental practice to:

- stamina
- speed
- precision
- timing
- subdivision
- grip and stroke consistency
- accent/tap contrast
- left/right balance

It also makes a critical point for drum-set players: the value is not only in reproducing the rudiment itself, but in transferring the technique, feel, articulation, and control to the kit.

Implication:

- the app should position triads and patterns as means to build transferable musical control, not as collectible pattern trivia

### 3. Linear drumming is a coordination and orchestration system

Drumeo defines linear drumming simply: limbs do not strike at the same time. Its teaching material links linear playing to:

- independence
- texture
- dynamic contrast
- coordination
- orchestration across different surfaces

Drumeo's linear curriculum also uses a progressive ladder:

- basic groove
- add ghost notes
- add more bass drum
- add doubles
- add triplets and other note-value changes
- add hi-hat foot
- combine techniques

Implication:

- linear playing should be taught as a progression from clean single-surface control into orchestrated, voiced drum-set movement
- ghost notes and kick integration are not random add-ons; they are staged expansions of the same language

### 4. Groupings create phrasing, tension, and new fill/groove ideas

Drumeo's grouping material makes several useful points:

- sticking patterns create phrasing ideas many drummers underuse
- artificial groupings change how a phrase feels even when the note count is fixed
- built-in accents materially change the sound and feel of the phrase
- the same pattern becomes new vocabulary when moved around the kit
- patterns become more musical when learned first in isolation, then internalized, then orchestrated

Drumeo's intermediate fill guidance also highlights odd groupings like `3`, `5`, and `7` as important ways to break out of predictable fill language.

Implication:

- triad and 5-note systems are not just technical drills
- they are engines for phrase shape, tension/release, and bar-line awareness

### 5. Weak-hand lead work changes both control and musical options

Drumeo's weak-hand and ambidexterity material consistently points to two payoffs:

- improved control and confidence in the non-dominant side
- more orchestration options and different musical ideas

The teaching logic is important:

- leading with the weaker hand is not only a fairness exercise
- it changes what comes out musically

Implication:

- the app should treat opposite-hand lead as both a technical and a musical category
- it should not collapse weak-hand work into generic "coverage"

### 6. Ghost notes are fundamentally dynamic/touch instruction

Drumeo describes ghost notes as quiet notes used to add dynamics and texture and even says they are often "more felt than heard."

PAS materials on strokes, accents, flams, and stick heights likewise reinforce that dynamic differentiation is a stroke-type problem, not just a notation problem.

Implication:

- accents and ghost notes should be treated as touch training
- they belong after basic pattern control, not before it
- they should influence coaching and practice recommendations, not just display

### 7. Flams are core rudimental material, but they are a different family of problem

PAS and Drumeo both treat flams as essential rudimental vocabulary. PAS specifically frames flam rudiments as an application of the basic stroke system, while Drumeo includes flams among the rudiments drummers should know and apply on the drum set.

But flams introduce a different problem than plain linear triad work:

- grace-note/main-note relationships
- widened attacks
- orchestration with ornamentation rather than simple stroke sequencing

Implication:

- flams are important
- but they should not be forced into the same conceptual box as plain triad and linear-grouping study
- they are better introduced as a later expansion family unless the app explicitly broadens its core scope

## Synthesis: What Triads, Combos, and Linear Patterns Actually Develop

This section is synthesis based on the sources above and on drum-set teaching logic.

### Triads

Triads are compact three-note sticking cells. Their value is not merely that they are short. Their value is that they are short enough to be:

- memorized quickly
- repeated many times without cognitive overload
- flipped to opposite-hand lead
- accented in multiple ways
- orchestrated onto the kit
- chained into longer phrases

What triads develop:

- **timing and subdivision**
  because they must be repeated evenly and resolved cleanly against the pulse
- **control**
  because short cells expose uneven stroke heights, uneven rebounds, and uneven hand quality
- **speed**
  because small repeating cells are natural vehicles for controlled repetition
- **stamina**
  because repetition reveals inefficient movement and weak-link fatigue
- **touch and dynamics**
  because accent/tap contrast becomes obvious very quickly in three-note cells
- **weak-hand development**
  because left-leading and right-leading variants are easy to compare directly
- **coordination**
  especially once `K` is introduced and the cell stays linear
- **bar-line awareness**
  because repeating 3-note cells across common meter naturally creates displacement
- **kit flow**
  because a short cell can be moved across surfaces without losing its identity

### Triad combinations

A triad combo is where triad work stops being isolated vocabulary and starts becoming phrase vocabulary.

What combos develop:

- **retention**
  because the player must remember order, not just one cell
- **transition control**
  because the drummer must move from one sticking logic to another without collapsing time
- **phrase shaping**
  because combinations create longer contour and tension/release
- **endurance under changing demands**
  because repeated transitions are more taxing than repeating one cell
- **fill vocabulary**
  because combos are closer to the way drummers actually move through a fill
- **soloing language**
  because chained cells can be sequenced, displaced, orchestrated, and extended

The app should therefore not treat combos as merely "saved lists." They are an intermediate teaching stage between isolated cell control and musical phrase usage.

### Linear patterns

Linear patterns are where the app becomes drum-set relevant.

What linear work develops:

- **independence and coordination**
  because the limbs must interlock cleanly without collisions
- **clarity of limb role**
  because each note belongs to a specific limb and voice
- **dynamic layering**
  because accents, ghosts, and kick placement create contour
- **orchestration**
  because the same sticking acquires different musical identity on different surfaces
- **groove vocabulary**
  because linear phrasing can become grooves, not just fills
- **improvisation**
  because once the player owns the cell mechanically, it can be moved, inverted, displaced, or voice-shifted in real time

### 5-note groupings

Five-note groupings are valuable, but pedagogically they should follow triads, not precede them.

Why:

- they create stronger phrase displacement
- they require better pulse retention
- they create more obvious over-the-bar-line tension
- they are more demanding as repeated cells

They are best taught after triads establish:

- evenness
- lead-hand flexibility
- accent/tap control
- basic kick integration

### Where rudiments fit

Rudiments should not replace the triad/linear system.

They fit in three ways:

1. **technical substrate**
   single strokes, doubles, accents, diddles, flams, drags, and stroke types explain why certain triads or combos feel difficult
2. **parallel vocabulary family**
   rudiments offer a broader historical vocabulary that can later be mapped to kit applications
3. **embellishment/expansion layer**
   once players own triads and linear sequencing, rudimental ideas can expand the language

Recommendation from this synthesis:

- keep the app centered on triads, triad combos, 5-note groupings, and linear application
- include rudiments as supporting context and later expansion
- keep flams out of the core v1 workflow, but design the product so an "embellished patterns" family can exist later

## Teaching Philosophy

This section is recommendation.

### Core principle

The app should teach **transferable musical control**, not just pattern exposure.

That means every concept should move through this ladder:

1. **recognize it**
2. **play it evenly**
3. **lead it both ways**
4. **shape it dynamically**
5. **repeat it without tension**
6. **chain it into phrases**
7. **move it around the kit**
8. **use it musically**

If a feature does not support one of those steps, it is probably secondary.

### What should be taught first

#### Stage 1. Hands-only triads

Start with hands-only triads before kick-based or full linear kit work.

Why:

- fewer moving parts
- easier to hear unevenness
- easier to compare right-leading vs left-leading
- easier to teach accent/tap contrast

The initial goal is not "learn the matrix." It is:

- build even time
- build balanced lead work
- recognize cell identity by feel and sound

#### Stage 2. Lead symmetry

Teach the same material with both right and left lead very early.

Why:

- it exposes imbalance quickly
- it prevents the whole system from becoming right-hand biased
- it expands phrase options later on the kit

#### Stage 3. Dynamic shape

Only after the cell is controlled should the app emphasize:

- accents
- taps
- ghost notes

The player must first hear and execute a stable base pulse. Otherwise dynamics become decorative confusion.

#### Stage 4. Kick integration

Bring `K` in only after hands-only control is established.

Why:

- kick inclusion changes balance, recovery, and phrase feel
- early kick inclusion can hide hand problems

#### Stage 5. Chaining and phrase length

Then move from one cell to:

- repeated cell loops
- two-cell combos
- longer combinations
- 5-note groupings

This is the moment where the product starts teaching phrasing rather than isolated control.

#### Stage 6. Kit application

After the player owns the phrase mechanically:

- map voices to surfaces
- move accents between voices
- treat the phrase as groove, fill, or solo fragment

This should be a distinct teaching step, not an automatic byproduct.

### Recommended progression model

#### Beginner within this topic

Beginner does not mean beginner drummer. It means beginner to this vocabulary.

Focus:

- hands-only triads
- right lead and left lead
- short sessions
- slow metronome work
- simple accent introduction
- recognition of doubles vs non-doubles

#### Intermediate within this topic

Focus:

- complete matrix coverage
- accent variations
- ghost-note control
- kick-containing cells
- two-cell and three-cell combos
- odd-grouping awareness through repeated 3s and early 5s
- first real kit orchestrations

#### Advanced within this topic

Focus:

- phrase displacement
- longer combos
- rapid lead switching
- dynamic orchestration
- groove creation from pattern material
- improvisational movement
- later expansion into rudimental embellishments, flams, and mixed vocabularies

### How single-surface work should relate to kit application

Single-surface work is not a separate product. It is the control lab for later drum-set transfer.

The relationship should be explicit:

- **single surface** teaches timing, symmetry, stroke quality, and dynamic contrast
- **kit application** teaches voice assignment, movement, phrase shape, and musical use

The app should show that relationship clearly. It should never imply that pad work is the end goal.

### How accents, ghost notes, handedness, and orchestration should be introduced

#### Handedness

Introduce immediately.

What changes:

- recommendation logic
- lead-balance analysis
- copy on Today
- technique filters in Matrix

What should not change:

- the core vocabulary itself

#### Accents

Introduce after the learner can loop a cell evenly.

Start with:

- single accented note within a cell
- repeated accent placement
- accent/tap contrast

#### Ghost notes

Introduce after accents.

Ghosts are subtler and more musical. They make more sense once the player already understands dynamic tiering.

#### Orchestration

Introduce only after identity is stable on one surface.

Otherwise the player changes surfaces before the phrase is internally learned.

### What coverage should mean

Coverage should be multi-dimensional.

It should not mean "has touched every triad once."

Recommended coverage dimensions:

1. **inventory coverage**
   which cells have ever been practiced
2. **lead coverage**
   right-leading vs left-leading exposure
3. **dynamic coverage**
   plain, accented, ghosted
4. **mechanical coverage**
   hands-only, kick-containing, doubles, starts/ends with kick
5. **phrase coverage**
   isolated cells vs combos vs longer phrases
6. **musical coverage**
   single-surface only vs orchestrated/kit-applied
7. **retention coverage**
   practiced once vs revisited and retained

That coverage model is much stronger than a flat minutes counter.

### What the app should optimize for

In order:

1. **retention**
   material should come back before it disappears
2. **transfer**
   pad work should feed kit playing
3. **musicality**
   dynamics, phrase shape, and orchestration should matter
4. **balanced development**
   opposite-hand lead and neglected categories should be surfaced
5. **routine building**
   the app should help the drummer maintain focus over time
6. **mastery**
   mastery matters, but only after the player is working on the right things

The product should not optimize first for:

- catalog completion
- arbitrary streaks
- decorative personalization

## Today Screen: What Real Analysis Should Look At

### Current issue

The current Today system has some real data hooks, but its framing is still partly fixed:

- the headline is fixed
- the summary is fixed
- cue titles are fixed
- cue generation is shallow

That makes it feel authored rather than earned.

### Recommended analysis angles

The app should score today's recommendations across several angles.

#### 1. Neglect

Questions:

- which items have not been touched in the longest time?
- which items are in the routine but have not been practiced recently?

Why it matters:

- retention requires re-exposure

#### 2. Weak-side imbalance

Questions:

- how much practice time has gone to right-leading vs left-leading material?
- how much of the routine is biased toward the dominant side?

Why it matters:

- bilateral learning is central to this topic

#### 3. Time vs confidence mismatch

Questions:

- which items have high logged time but still low competency?
- which items are marked comfortable/reliable but have not been revisited recently?

Why it matters:

- exposes stuck material and overestimated material

#### 4. Coverage gaps

Questions:

- which technical categories are under-covered?
- hands-only vs kick
- doubles vs non-doubles
- right lead vs left lead
- accented vs unaccented
- triads vs combos vs 5s

Why it matters:

- prevents narrow practice habits

#### 5. Near-mastery opportunities

Questions:

- which items are close to reliable or musical?
- which items have enough recent work that one more focused push may move them forward?

Why it matters:

- momentum matters psychologically

#### 6. Routine continuity

Questions:

- which routine items have gone stale?
- which routine items are getting all the attention while others are ignored?

Why it matters:

- routine should be a working queue, not a storage bin

#### 7. Recency balance

Questions:

- how repetitive has recent practice been?
- has the player been doing only one family or one type of mechanical problem?

Why it matters:

- a good session should balance familiarity and freshness

#### 8. Dynamic underdevelopment

Questions:

- which items are practiced plain but not with accent work?
- which are practiced accented but never ghosted?

Why it matters:

- dynamics are central to musicality in this domain

#### 9. Phrase development

Questions:

- is the player mostly doing isolated cells?
- are combos and longer phrases underrepresented?

Why it matters:

- the app should move players toward phrase language, not trap them in flash-card mode

### Recommended recommendation ranking model

Today should not simply list categories. It should rank opportunities.

Suggested scoring model:

`priority = retention + imbalance + stagnation + coverage gap + routine relevance + momentum`

Practical interpretation:

- **retention**
  long time since last practice
- **imbalance**
  weak-side or category underexposure
- **stagnation**
  lots of time, little competency change
- **coverage gap**
  missing category exposure
- **routine relevance**
  currently marked as important by the user
- **momentum**
  close to a meaningful milestone

Recommended Today output:

1. **Primary focus**
   the single highest-priority lane for today
2. **Reinforcement**
   one near-mastery or routine-continuity target
3. **Fresh exposure**
   one neglected or under-covered item

That structure feels coached rather than random.

## First-Light / Onboarding Voice

### Current issue

The current onboarding copy reads like product explanation from the builder's point of view:

- "This app helps you..."
- "Tap triads in the matrix..."
- "Use filters when you want..."

It explains interface behavior before it establishes the musical journey.

### Recommended voice

The voice should feel like:

- calm
- clear
- teacherly
- specific
- musically serious
- not chatty
- not self-aware
- not startup-like

It should sound like a thoughtful drum teacher setting a direction.

### Voice principles

1. **lead with musical purpose**
   before interface explanation
2. **speak in terms of practice outcomes**
   not feature mechanics
3. **be concise**
   avoid overexplaining
4. **sound confident, not hype-driven**
5. **treat the learner as a musician**
   even when they are new to the topic

### Copy examples

#### Welcome

Not this:

- "This app helps you build triad vocabulary into your hands."

Closer to this:

- "Triads and short linear groupings can sharpen control, balance, touch, and phrase vocabulary. This app helps you build that language in a structured way."

#### Setup

- "Set your defaults so practice starts quickly."
- "Choose your lead side and a comfortable starting tempo."
- "You can change these any time."

#### Practice guidance

- "Start with even sound and relaxed motion."
- "Then match the phrase with the opposite lead."
- "Add dynamic shape only after the pattern feels steady."

#### Review/progress

- "This material is settling in."
- "This side is underworked."
- "This phrase has time in it, but not consistency yet."

The copy should evaluate the practice state, not narrate the UI.

## Product Critique

This section critiques the app conceptually as it exists now.

### What is aligned

#### The matrix is becoming the right center of gravity

This is the strongest part of the product right now.

Why it works:

- it makes the vocabulary visible
- it can act as both graph and instrument
- it supports discovery, filtering, and selection
- it has the beginnings of a real teaching surface

#### Accents and ghosts are becoming first-class material

This is directionally correct. Dynamic marking is essential for touch, articulation, and musical relevance.

#### Handedness and lead-direction awareness are in the right neighborhood

The product is correctly moving away from generic "level" ideas and toward side-specific analysis.

#### Direct practice launch is better than a generic setup form

Removing the broad setup screen was the right move. Practice needs fewer admin steps.

### What feels patchy or off-model

#### Today is still authored more than analyzed

The data model supports some analysis, but the experience still reads like:

- fixed headline
- fixed summary
- fixed cue slots

It does not yet feel like a live teacher looking at your actual work.

#### The product still overindexes on storage views

Toolkit and Progress still feel closer to categorized lists than to teaching tools.

Examples:

- routine is mostly a list
- progress is mostly a dashboard
- combos are mostly saved objects

The teaching model suggests these should feel more like:

- active assignments
- development lanes
- phrase pathways

#### The app does not yet clearly separate learning stages

Right now the user can move between:

- single triads
- combos
- custom patterns
- technique filters

But the product does not yet make the progression explicit:

- control
- symmetry
- dynamics
- kick integration
- phrase building
- orchestration

That is a core reason it feels unfocused.

#### The domain still contains some pre-kit abstractions that are not earning their keep

Examples:

- `ComboIntentTagV1`
- some family/category distinctions that matter to storage more than to learning

These concepts can stay internally, but they should not dominate the teaching flow unless they correspond to a real instructional stage.

### What should be removed or downgraded

- hard-coded Today framing
- onboarding copy that explains the app before explaining the practice goal
- any "progress" view that is only a passive ledger
- concepts that imply kit/flow sophistication before orchestration is truly taught

### What should be postponed

- flam-centric expansion
- broad rudiment catalog integration
- elaborate kit-return math for fills
- advanced groove-resolution systems

These are valid later topics, but they should not compete with building a coherent triad/linear core.

### What should become central

1. the matrix as the primary learning surface
2. a real analysis engine that drives Today
3. progression stages from cell to phrase to kit application
4. explicit handling of lead balance, dynamic shape, and phrase coverage
5. routine as an active working set, not just a saved list

## Alignment Plan

### Stage A: Clarify the teaching spine

Before more feature work, the app should define explicit teaching lanes:

1. **Control**
   even single-surface execution
2. **Balance**
   right lead / left lead symmetry
3. **Dynamics**
   accents and ghosts
4. **Integration**
   kick-containing linear cells
5. **Phrasing**
   combos and 5-note groupings
6. **Application**
   kit orchestration and groove/fill usage

Every major screen should map to one or more of these lanes.

### Stage B: Screen-level recommendations

#### Today

Today should answer three questions:

1. what deserves attention now?
2. why?
3. what should I do first?

Recommended structure:

- **Primary Assignment**
  one lane for the day
- **Support Work**
  one near-ready or routine item
- **Fresh Material**
  one neglected or under-covered item
- **Why These**
  short evidence-based copy

Remove the feeling of generic inspiration. Make it diagnostic and specific.

#### Matrix

Matrix should be the core teaching surface.

It should do four jobs:

1. show the vocabulary
2. reveal analytical overlays
3. allow selection/building
4. communicate category structure

Recommended additions:

- a visible stage lens:
  `Control`, `Balance`, `Dynamics`, `Integration`, `Phrasing`
- clearer explanation of what each palette means in learning terms
- eventually, a way to switch between plain cells and orchestrated application views

The matrix should become the place where the student sees:

- what exists
- what is weak
- what is missing
- what is ready

#### Toolkit

Toolkit should become **Working Set**, not just storage.

Recommended structure:

- `Current Work`
- `Nearly Ready`
- `Saved Combos`
- `Custom Patterns`

Each item should answer:

- why it is here
- when it was last touched
- what the next useful action is

#### Progress

Progress should stop being mostly descriptive and become evaluative.

Recommended sections:

- `Retention`
- `Balance`
- `Coverage`
- `Milestones`

The most useful progress questions are:

- what is sticking?
- what is stale?
- what is one-sided?
- what categories are missing?

#### Practice Session

Practice Session should teach one thing at a time.

Recommended session framing:

- show the target lane
- show the main technical instruction
- keep the phrase visually dominant
- make accent/ghost editing available
- later: show orchestration/application prompts when the app reaches that stage

The session UI should feel less like a control panel and more like a focused drill page.

### Stage C: Data model implications

The current data model is usable, but a stronger Today engine and teaching model will need more explicit analytical fields.

Recommended additions:

- per-item last practiced timestamp
- per-item total session count
- lead-category aggregates
- dynamic-variation aggregates
- category flags for:
  - hands only
  - has kick
  - starts with kick
  - ends with kick
  - doubles
  - pure triples
  - accent-capable
- session intent or lane:
  - control
  - balance
  - dynamics
  - integration
  - phrasing
  - application
- recommendation evidence payload for Today cards

Recommended possible future model:

- `LearningLane`
- `CoverageSnapshot`
- `RecommendationReason`
- `ApplicationPattern`

### Stage D: What happens next, later, much later

#### Next

1. remove demo/mock startup data from normal boot
2. rewrite onboarding in teacher voice
3. redesign Today around real recommendation scoring
4. redefine Toolkit as working set / current assignments
5. document learning lanes in the UI

#### Later

1. add explicit dynamic coverage analysis
2. add phrase-coverage analysis
3. add early orchestration prompts
4. add 5-note grouping progression as a distinct lane
5. add first layer of rudiment mapping where it helps explain technique

#### Much later

1. add embellishment families:
   flams, drags, rudimental hybrids
2. add deeper kit application systems
3. add groove-return / phrase-resolution systems
4. add more sophisticated orchestration and voicing analytics

## Recommended Immediate Product Decisions

These are the next decisions worth making with the product owner.

1. Is the core promise of the app:
   `own the matrix`, or `turn short patterns into musical vocabulary`?
   My recommendation: the second.

2. Is `5-note grouping` a first-class lane in v1, or an expansion after triads?
   My recommendation: expansion after triads.

3. Are flams in core scope?
   My recommendation: no, not in the core v1 flow; prepare the system for them later.

4. Should Today recommend a broad menu, or a small ranked assignment?
   My recommendation: one primary assignment plus one support and one fresh exposure item.

5. Should Toolkit remain a library label?
   My recommendation: no. Rename or reposition it as a working set / assignments surface.
